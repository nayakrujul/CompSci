GCSE Link: 4.13 - Magnetic Storage

Secondary storage is non-volatile storage which isn't directly accessible by the CPU.

Computer systems need secondary storage, as RAM is volatile (so can't be used for long-term storage) and ROM is read-only.

There are three types of secondary storage: magnetic, flash and optical.


Hard Disk Drives (HDDs) are the most common form of magnetic storage in computers.

HDDs use magnetised regions on metal discs called platters to store binary data. These platters spin at up to 10,000 revolutions per minute. Each platter is divided into tracks and sectors, which allows the physical location of the data to be identified. A magnetic arm is moved across the platter to read or write data.

Hard disks offer high capacity and low cost-per-GB, however they can be damaged by large impacts as they contain moving parts.


Solid State Drives (SSDs) are the most common form of flash storage in computers.

They use NAND flash memory cells to store data using electrical charges. Cells are arranged into pages, and pages are arranged into blocks. The controller chip manages the reading and writing of data: data is written in pages but erased in blocks.

SSDs offer faster transfer speeds and lower latency than HDDs, as well as being more robust as they have no moving parts. This makes them ideal for portable devices like laptops and mobile phones. However, they can be more expensive. Also, NAND memory degrades over time, meaning that SSDs have a limited number of read/write cycles before they become unreliable.


Optical Storage uses indentations on the surface of a disc to represent binary data.

To read the disc, a reader shines laser light at the surface as the disc spins. The intensity of reflected light is then measured to detect the pits and lands, which are then converted into binary.

The three main types of optical storage are CDs (which hold around 700MB of data, ideal for audio files), DVDs (which hold around 4.7GB of data, ideal for movies), and Blu-ray discs (which hold around 25GB of data, ideal for software such as large games).

Each type comes in three different formats: ROM (read-only memory: data on the disc cannot be changed), R (recordable: data can be written once and then can't be changed), and RW (rewritable: data can be rewritten multiple times).

While optical storage is very cheap and portable, it has a low capacity and slow read/write speeds. Discs can also be easily damaged by scratches.


Which type of optical storage disc is the most suitable for 4K video distribution?

Blu-ray ROM, as CDs and DVDs will not have sufficient capacity for large 4K video files, and the video does not need to be rewritten.
